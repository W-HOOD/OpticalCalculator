The elevation angle ε of light deflected within a water droplet is plotted against the frequency f of light. The lower curve corresponds to the primary rainbow, while the upper curve represents the secondary rainbow.
These elevation angles correspond to the minima and maxima of light deflection within the droplet, indicating focused regions where light is concentrated. These focused angles correspond to the rainbow’s maximum observed intensity.
Curve colours represent their corresponding colours within the visible spectrum., illustrating how dispersion causes the angular separation of colors in rainbows.
The refraction angle φ of light inside a water droplet is plotted as a function of frequency f . The two lower curves represent the light paths for the primary and secondary rainbows, respectively. These angles correspond to the internal refraction at the droplet’s back surface just before the final exit.
The upper black curve shows the critical angle for total internal reflection, above which light is completely reflected within the droplet. Since the rainbow paths lie below this threshold, some light escapes the droplet at each internal reflection, resulting in intensity loss — especially for the secondary rainbow, which undergoes more reflections.
This chart visualizes how refractive index dispersion affects the internal light path and highlights the physical limit imposed by total internal reflection in shaping the brightness and formation of rainbows.
The elevation angle ε for each colour is used to render primary and secondary rainbows on a coordinate grid. The solar angle α determines how much of a rainbow is visible to a viewer at horizon.
